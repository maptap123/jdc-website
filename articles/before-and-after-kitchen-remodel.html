Quick Answer

Most before and after kitchen remodel projects in Southeast Indiana take 6 to 12 weeks on site and run $40K to $120K+, depending on how much you're moving. The biggest visual jumps come from changing layout, lighting, and cabinet style — not from buying the most expensive finishes.

What Actually Drives the "Wow" in a Before-and-After

Most homeowners assume the difference between a so-so remodel and a stunning one comes down to budget. It doesn't. After almost 30 years of remodeling kitchens around Lawrenceburg and Dearborn County, the biggest visual changes always come from four things: layout, light, color, and continuity.

A small kitchen with smart layout changes will outperform a big kitchen with expensive finishes and the same awkward walls. Light alone (under-cabinet, recessed, pendants over the island) changes how every other surface reads. A consistent story across cabinet, counter, floor, and hardware makes the room feel designed instead of decorated.

Counter and backsplash usually get the credit in the after photo. They're not wrong, but they're the finish, not the foundation.

Three Real Transformation Tiers

Most before and after kitchen remodel projects in Southeast Indiana fall into one of three ranges. The price difference is mostly about how much you're moving — walls, plumbing, electrical, structure — not the finish materials themselves.

Tier 1
Cosmetic Refresh

Same layout. New cabinets or refacing, new counters, backsplash, lighting, paint. Big visual change without moving walls. Best for kitchens where the footprint already works.

$25K–$50K 4–7 weeks
Tier 2
Mid-Range Pull and Replace

Full gut. New cabinets, counters, appliances, flooring, lighting. Some minor electrical and plumbing relocation. The most common JDC kitchen project.

$55K–$90K 8–12 weeks
Tier 3
Layout-Changing Remodel

Walls move. Island goes in. Often an open-concept conversion that pulls in the dining or living area. Custom cabinets, premium counters, structural work.

$90K–$160K+ 12–18 weeks

How Long the "After" Actually Takes

The honest answer: it depends on what you're changing. Demo is fast. Decisions are slow. Most of the timeline is rough-in trades, cabinet lead times, and the punch list at the end.

  • Weeks 1–2: Demo, framing changes, structural updates if needed.
  • Weeks 2–4: Rough plumbing, electrical, and HVAC. Inspections in between.
  • Weeks 4–6: Drywall, paint, flooring underlayment.
  • Weeks 6–9: Cabinet install, counter template and fab, tile and backsplash.
  • Weeks 9–12: Appliances, plumbing trim, lighting, hardware, punch list.

"The kitchens that photograph best aren't always the most expensive. They're the ones where every choice was made on purpose."

What Surprises Homeowners the Most

The before-and-after that lands hardest is rarely about the finish package. It's about what changes underneath. A wall that comes down. A window that gets added. A run of cabinets that finally goes to the ceiling.

The other surprise is how much the lighting plan does. We've watched the same exact cabinets and counters look like a different kitchen once we layered in under-cabinet, recessed, and pendant lighting on dimmers. Most older Southeast Indiana kitchens were built with one ceiling fixture and called done. Fixing that is one of the cheapest ways to make a remodel feel premium.

The third thing: dust and noise. Even with zip walls and daily cleanup, a kitchen remodel disrupts the heart of the house. Plan for it. Most of our kitchen remodeling clients set up a temporary kitchen in the garage, basement, or dining room.

How to Plan a Kitchen Remodel That Photographs Well

If you want a before-and-after that genuinely looks like two different kitchens, plan around these decisions before you pick a single tile.

  • Decide on layout first. Before finishes, before cabinets, before counters. Layout drives everything else.
  • Plan three layers of light. Ambient (recessed), task (under-cabinet), and accent (pendant or sconce). Put each on its own switch.
  • Pick one hero material. A statement counter, a bold backsplash, or a wood ceiling. Then dial the rest back to support it.
  • Take cabinets to the ceiling. Or trim out the gap with crown. The dead space above standard cabinets is the most aging detail in a kitchen.
  • Match the floor to the next room. Continuity reads as intentional. A different floor at the kitchen threshold reads as dated.
  • Hold one detail back. Pick the hardware or the lighting last, after the cabinet color and counter are finalized. It's the punctuation.

For most homeowners in the Cincinnati tri-state area, the biggest mistake we see is sequencing: finishes get picked before the floor plan is locked in, and the plan ends up serving the materials instead of the other way around.

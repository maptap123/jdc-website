A kitchen remodel is one of the best investments you can make in your home, but the cost only makes sense once you understand what the work actually involves. The two go hand in hand, and planning both together is how you avoid surprises.

Quick Answer

A full kitchen remodel in Southeast Indiana typically costs $30,000 to $80,000 and runs 6 to 12 weeks of construction. The number is driven by your finishes, whether you change the layout, and the condition of the plumbing and wiring behind the walls.

What a kitchen remodel actually includes

A kitchen remodel is more than new cabinets and countertops. It's a coordinated run of trades: demolition, plumbing, electrical, drywall, flooring, cabinetry, counters, and finish work, all happening in a set order.

That sequence is what shapes both the timeline and the cost. The more of those systems you touch, the higher the number climbs.

In Southeast Indiana, most of the kitchens we remodel sit in homes built decades ago. Older wiring, settled subfloors, and outdated plumbing are common in Lawrenceburg and Dearborn County, and what we find behind the walls often shapes the final price.

How much does a kitchen remodel cost?

Most full kitchen remodels in the Lawrenceburg and Cincinnati area run $30,000 to $80,000. Where you land comes down to materials, layout changes, and the shape of what's already there.

Here's how the cost usually breaks into tiers:

Tier 1
Cosmetic Refresh

Cabinet refacing or paint, entry-level quartz or laminate counters, new fixtures, and vinyl plank flooring. Same layout, no plumbing or electrical moves.

$30K–$45K All-in
Tier 2
Full Replacement

All-new cabinets, quartz or granite counters, tile backsplash, new appliances, and updated lighting and electrical. Same footprint.

$45K–$65K All-in
Tier 3
Layout & Custom

Wall removal, relocated plumbing and gas, custom cabinetry, premium counters, and hardwood or tile floors. Often opens the kitchen to a living or dining area.

$70K–$110K+ All-in

The jump between tiers is rarely about the finishes alone. It's about how much structure, plumbing, and electrical you disturb to get there.

What moves the price up or down

Two kitchens the same size can cost wildly different amounts. These are the factors that decide which end of the range you land on.

  • Layout changes. Moving a sink, range, or wall means new plumbing, electrical, and framing. This is the single biggest cost driver.
  • Cabinets. Stock, semi-custom, and full-custom cabinetry can swing the budget by $15,000 or more on its own.
  • Countertops. Laminate, quartz, granite, and natural stone each sit at a different price point, and the size of your runs matters.
  • What's behind the walls. Old knob-and-tube wiring or failing supply lines in an older Indiana home have to be brought up to code.
  • Appliances. A mid-range package and a high-end built-in package can differ by tens of thousands.

The cost of a kitchen remodel isn't set by the finishes you can see. It's set by everything you don't, behind the walls and under the floor.

Where the money actually goes

Knowing how the budget splits up helps you decide where to spend and where to hold back. The proportions stay fairly consistent across most projects we run.

The big line items

Cabinets and counters together usually eat 40 to 50 percent of a kitchen budget. Labor runs another 20 to 35 percent, since a remodel is trade-heavy and the work has to happen in sequence.

Appliances, flooring, lighting, plumbing fixtures, and finishes make up the rest. Permits and a contingency for the unexpected should always have a line of their own.

Set aside 10 to 15 percent of your total for surprises. In an older Dearborn County home, the wall almost always has a story to tell once it's open.

How to plan your kitchen remodel and budget

The homeowners who stay on budget are the ones who plan the cost and the scope at the same time. Start here.

  • Decide your tier first. Pick a realistic budget range before you fall in love with finishes you can't fit into it.
  • Keep the layout if you can. Holding the existing footprint is the fastest way to keep the number down without sacrificing quality.
  • Spend where you touch it daily. Cabinets, counters, and lighting earn their cost. Splurge appliances rarely do.
  • Build in a contingency. A 10 to 15 percent buffer turns a surprise into a non-event.
  • Get a fixed scope in writing. A clear contract is what keeps the final invoice close to the estimate.
